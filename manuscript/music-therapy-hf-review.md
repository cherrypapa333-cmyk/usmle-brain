## Abstract (placeholder — to be drafted last by lead author, ~250 words)

*Background:* Heart failure (HF) carries a high symptom burden that is incompletely addressed by guideline-directed medical therapy. *Objective:* To synthesize evidence from the last decade on music as an adjunctive therapy in HF, with attention to mechanisms, clinical outcomes, and implementation. *Methods:* Narrative review of six representative studies spanning conceptual frameworks, RCTs, pilot trials, and physiological investigations. *Results:* Across home-care, inpatient, and post-discharge contexts, music interventions produced consistent improvements in sleep, anxiety, depression, fatigue, cognition, and disease-specific quality of life, with parallel signals of autonomic rebalancing and reduced inflammatory tone. *Conclusion:* Music therapy is a low-risk, low-cost adjunct with biologically plausible mechanisms and a maturing evidence base, but standardization, scalability, and long-term outcome data remain open questions.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1. Epidemiology and Burden of Heart Failure

Heart failure is one of the most common cardiovascular syndromes worldwide, affecting an estimated 64 million people globally and accounting for a disproportionate share of hospital admissions, readmissions, and healthcare expenditure.<sup>1</sup> Beyond mortality, patients live with a chronic burden of breathlessness, fatigue, and exercise intolerance that is layered with high rates of anxiety, depression, and disturbed sleep. These non-cardiac symptoms are not incidental — they track with worse adherence, more readmissions, and poorer self-rated quality of life (QOL).

### 1.2. Limitations of Current Treatments

Modern HF pharmacotherapy — renin-angiotensin system inhibitors, beta-blockers, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, and SGLT2 inhibitors — has reshaped survival but was not designed to address mood, sleep, or psychosocial functioning. Device therapies and structured cardiac rehabilitation help selected patients, yet uptake is uneven and access is often limited by geography, comorbidity, or cost. The result is a therapeutic gap: patients are kept alive longer but continue to feel unwell. Clinical guidelines increasingly call for integrated, holistic care, but practical, scalable non-pharmacologic options remain underdeveloped.

### 1.3. Rationale for Music Therapy in HF

Music is an attractive candidate for filling that gap. It is inexpensive, broadly acceptable, non-invasive, and can be delivered in the hospital, the home, or virtually. Mechanistically, listening to music engages overlapping neural, autonomic, endocrine, and immune circuits — exactly the circuits dysregulated in advanced HF. The aim of this review is to synthesize evidence from the last decade on how music acts in HF, what clinical benefits are supported, and what is needed to translate these findings into routine care.

> *Transition:* Before evaluating clinical trials, we first situate music's effects within a unifying biological framework, since this framework explains why interventions as different as nightly nature sounds and three-month classical-music protocols converge on similar outcomes.

## 2. Biological Mechanisms: How Music Influences HF Pathophysiology

### 2.1. The PNIE (Psychoneuroimmunoendocrinological) Framework

The psychoneuroimmunoendocrinological (PNIE) model, articulated for HF specifically by Burrai and colleagues, links music's perceptual effects to measurable downstream changes in nervous, endocrine, and immune signaling.<sup>2</sup> It is useful precisely because it lets disparate outcomes — heart rate variability, cortisol, IL-6, cognition, depression scores — be read as facets of a single integrated response rather than as unrelated findings.

### 2.2. Autonomic Nervous System Regulation

The most reproducible physiological signature is a shift toward parasympathetic dominance: higher vagally mediated heart rate variability (HRV) indices such as rMSSD and pNN50, and lower LF/HF ratios.<sup>3</sup> In a cohort of elderly patients with cardiovascular disease, including a subset with congestive HF, music therapy produced both HRV improvements and a reduction in CHF events over follow-up.<sup>3</sup> Clinically, this matters because reduced HRV is a well-established marker of adverse prognosis in HF, and any safe, low-cost lever that nudges autonomic balance in the right direction deserves attention.

### 2.3. Endocrine Modulation

Music listening is consistently associated with reductions in cortisol and circulating catecholamines (adrenaline, noradrenaline).<sup>3</sup> The implication is a dampening of the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal (HPA) axis and sympathoadrenal drive — both of which are pathologically elevated in HF and contribute to remodeling, vasoconstriction, and patient-reported fatigue.

### 2.4. Immune and Inflammatory Effects

HF is, in part, an inflammatory disease. Elevated IL-6 and related cytokines correlate with disease severity. Music interventions have been linked to reductions in IL-6, suggesting that the autonomic and endocrine effects above translate downstream into measurable immunomodulation.<sup>3</sup> Whether the magnitude is clinically meaningful in isolation is unclear; what matters is the directionality and consistency across mechanisms.

### 2.5. Neurocognitive Engagement

Music recruits the amygdala, hippocampus, and prefrontal cortex — circuits central to emotional regulation, memory, and executive function. Burrai et al. observed improvements in cognitive function alongside affective and somatic improvements in HF patients receiving daily classical music.<sup>4</sup> Given the high prevalence of "cardiogenic" cognitive complaints in HF, this domain deserves more deliberate study.

### 2.6. Entrainment and Behavioral Regulation

Slow-tempo music can entrain breathing and heart rate toward calmer patterns, an effect that has been leveraged to support sleep onset and relaxation.<sup>5</sup> Beyond the physiologic moment, embedding a daily listening ritual may also serve as a behavioral anchor — a cue that scaffolds medication-taking, weight monitoring, and other self-care tasks.

> *Transition:* These mechanistic threads predict that music should improve sleep, mood, and disease-specific QOL across delivery models. The clinical literature largely confirms that prediction.

## 3. Clinical Evidence of Music Therapy in Heart Failure

### 3.1. Randomized Controlled Trials

**Classical music for broad symptom relief.** The most ambitious trial in this space is the three-month home-based RCT by Burrai et al., which randomized 159 HF patients to 30 minutes per day of recorded classical music versus usual care.<sup>4</sup> The intervention arm showed statistically significant improvements in HF-specific QOL (P < 0.001), generic QOL, sleep (P = 0.007), anxiety, depression (both P < 0.001), and cognition (P = 0.003). The trial is notable for two reasons: the breadth of domains affected, and the demonstration that benefit can accrue in the patient's own home with minimal supervision.

**Nature music for inpatient sleep.** Hosseini et al. tackled a narrower but high-yield target — sleep in hospitalized HF patients — using 30 minutes of nature-themed music nightly for three nights via headphones.<sup>5</sup> They reported significant improvements in mental sleep quality (P < 0.001), daily function (P = 0.002), and reduced sedative use (P < 0.001). The intervention is short, simple, and directly addresses a problem (poor inpatient sleep) that worsens delirium risk and prolongs admissions.

Together these trials establish a useful range: a long, home-based protocol on one end, a brief, acute-care protocol on the other. Both produced clinically meaningful effects, suggesting that dose flexibility is real rather than illusory.

### 3.2. Pilot Studies and Hybrid Models

Yu et al. piloted a hybrid music therapy model in 20 patients with HF and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, combining two in-person and two virtual sessions across the post-discharge transition.<sup>6</sup> Retention was high (~85%) and patients reported reduced anxiety and improved engagement, but the team also surfaced barriers that any future deployment will face: difficulty with technology after discharge, communication gaps once patients leave the hospital, and the need for pre-discharge digital onboarding. The pilot is small but operationally instructive — it tells us not only that hybrid delivery is feasible, but where the implementation effort actually lives.

### 3.3. Physiological Studies

Okada et al. provided some of the most cited mechanistic data, studying 87 elderly patients with cardiovascular disease (including a CHF subset) across ten 45-minute music therapy sessions.<sup>3</sup> The investigators documented increased parasympathetic indices on HRV analysis, lower IL-6, lower adrenaline and noradrenaline, and fewer congestive events during follow-up. Although the design has limitations — single-center, modest size, mixed CV diagnoses — it remains an important bridge between the mechanistic claims of the PNIE model and downstream clinical events.

### 3.4. Symptom-Specific Studies

Najafi Ghezelejh et al. compared patient-preferred music with back massage in 90 hospitalized HF patients, using three 20-minute sessions.<sup>7</sup> Both interventions reduced fatigue significantly, but massage produced a larger effect. Read narrowly, this could be interpreted as a negative result for music. Read more usefully, it positions music as one component of a multimodal non-pharmacologic toolkit rather than a stand-alone fix — a framing that aligns with how complementary therapies are typically combined in real practice.

> *Transition:* The clinical signal is consistent across designs and outcomes. Returning to the PNIE framework helps explain why.

## 4. Mechanistic Basis: The PNIE Model in Practice

The PNIE model, as applied to HF, proposes that music acts simultaneously on three integrated axes:<sup>2</sup>

- **Neural:** modulation of autonomic balance via brainstem (nucleus tractus solitarius, vagal nuclei) and limbic circuits, shifting from sympathetic toward parasympathetic dominance.
- **Endocrine:** suppression of HPA-axis output, with measurable reductions in cortisol and catecholamines.
- **Immune:** downstream attenuation of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including IL-6.

A fourth, often under-appreciated, layer is **entrainment** — the tendency for cardiorespiratory rhythms to couple with musical tempo. This is the most likely proximal mechanism for music's acute effects on heart rate, respiratory rate, and subjective relaxation.

The clinical relevance is that the same intervention can plausibly improve sleep (via autonomic and entrainment effects), mood (via limbic and endocrine effects), and inflammatory tone (via the immune arm) without invoking separate mechanisms for each outcome. That parsimony is exactly what makes music a credible adjunct rather than a placebo curiosity.

> *Controversy to flag:* Critics argue that effect sizes in non-blinded behavioral interventions are inflated by attention and expectancy. The PNIE framework helps adjudicate this: if mechanistic biomarkers (HRV, cortisol, IL-6) move in parallel with subjective scales, a pure-expectancy explanation becomes harder to sustain.

## 5. Practical Considerations for Clinical Integration

### 5.1. Music Selection and Personalization

The trials cited here used classical music,<sup>4</sup> nature music,<sup>5</sup> and patient-preferred selections.<sup>7</sup> No head-to-head data privilege one genre, and cultural familiarity and personal preference appear to drive engagement. A pragmatic protocol allows patients to choose from a curated, tempo-controlled library rather than mandating a single genre.

### 5.2. Duration and Mode of Delivery

Effective protocols range from three nights of in-hospital listening<sup>5</sup> to three months of daily home-based sessions,<sup>4</sup> typically in 20–30-minute blocks. In-person delivery maximizes adherence but is resource-intensive; virtual and hybrid models extend reach but introduce technology-related drop-off.<sup>6</sup>

### 5.3. Role of Nursing and Care Teams

Most successful protocols have been led by nurses, who are well placed to embed listening sessions into existing care routines (sleep hygiene, pre-discharge teaching, cardiac rehabilitation). Training requirements are minimal; infrastructure can be as light as a headphone set and a curated playlist on a hospital tablet. The implication for institutions is that a low-capital pilot is realistic without specialty-trained music therapists, though credentialed therapists likely add value for more intensive or psychotherapeutic protocols.

### 5.4. Equity and Access

A non-trivial implementation concern is that virtual delivery presupposes device access, connectivity, and digital literacy. Hybrid models such as Yu et al.'s should be paired with hardware loans, simple text-based check-ins, and pre-discharge onboarding to avoid widening existing disparities.<sup>6</sup>

> *Transition:* These integration points lead naturally to the gaps that the next generation of trials must close.

## 6. Limitations and Future Directions

Several gaps recur across the literature:

1. **Sample size and power.** Most trials enroll fewer than 200 patients and are single-center. Multi-center RCTs powered for hard endpoints (readmission, mortality) are absent.
2. **Outcome standardization.** Sleep, fatigue, and QOL are measured with heterogeneous instruments, complicating meta-analysis.
3. **Protocol heterogeneity.** Genre, tempo, duration, frequency, and delivery mode vary widely. A consensus minimum dataset for music-therapy trials in HF would accelerate synthesis.
4. **Long-term follow-up.** Few studies extend beyond three months, leaving durability of benefit unknown.
5. **Mechanistic confirmation.** Biomarker measurement (HRV, cortisol, cytokines) is inconsistent across trials, limiting our ability to test the PNIE model rigorously.
6. **Digital integration.** Personalized playlists, wearable-derived HRV feedback, and app-based adherence prompts are obvious next steps but largely untested in HF cohorts.

A pragmatic research agenda for the next five years would include: (a) a multi-center RCT of an app-delivered, tempo-controlled, patient-personalized listening protocol with hard clinical endpoints; (b) embedded mechanistic substudies measuring HRV and inflammatory markers; and (c) implementation studies focused on equitable post-discharge access.

## 7. Conclusion

Music therapy occupies an unusual position in HF care: biologically plausible, clinically supported across diverse settings, virtually free of harm, and yet still peripheral to standard guidelines. The evidence reviewed here — a conceptual framework,<sup>2</sup> two RCTs,<sup>4,5</sup> a hybrid pilot,<sup>6</sup> a quasi-experimental fatigue trial,<sup>7</sup> and a physiological study with event data<sup>3</sup> — converges on a consistent picture: music modestly but reliably improves sleep, mood, fatigue, cognition, and disease-specific QOL, with parallel signals of autonomic rebalancing and reduced inflammation. The remaining work is largely translational: standardize protocols, scale through digital tools, demonstrate durability and effects on hard endpoints, and ensure equitable access. In the meantime, given the favorable risk profile and minimal cost, integrating brief, structured music listening into HF care pathways is a reasonable step that does not require waiting for definitive trials.
